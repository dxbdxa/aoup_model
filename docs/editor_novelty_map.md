## Editor-Facing Novelty Statement

The cleanest editor-facing novelty statement is:

> This work turns a one-maze optimization landscape into a falsifiable, geometry-tested, metric-robust pre-commit transport principle.

Why this is the right novelty line:

- `one-maze optimization landscape` names the baseline expectation the paper exceeds
- `falsifiable` is justified by the reduced-law prediction package
- `geometry-tested` is justified by GF0/GF1/GF2 shape transfer with coefficient renormalization
- `metric-robust` is justified by ridge survival across the current efficiency family
- `pre-commit transport principle` names the actual mechanism level the paper supports

## Novelty Ladder

If the editor asks what is new beyond a standard simulation optimization paper, answer in this order.

1. Numerical novelty: the productive region is a Pareto-like ridge with separated front tips, not a single optimum.
2. Mechanistic novelty: the decisive structure is already created before final crossing by a pre-commit backbone.
3. Theoretical novelty: the mechanism compresses into a reduced control principle in which delay is admissibility, memory is productive smoothing, and flow orders branch choice.
4. Generality novelty: the transferable object survives beyond the reference maze at the level of shape, while coefficients renormalize.
5. Robustness novelty: the principle survives a metric upgrade, so the ridge is not tied to one screening definition even though branch preference changes.

## What This Paper Is Stronger Than

Stronger than:

- one best-point optimization in one geometry
- descriptive scan phenomenology without a reduced mechanism
- one-geometry mechanism language with no transfer test
- one-metric efficiency language with no robustness check

Not yet stronger than:

- a full crossing-completion law
- unrestricted geometry universality
- coefficient-exact transport collapse
- full thermodynamic closure

## Editor-Safe Framing

Best editorial compression:

- `geometry-tested pre-commit principle`
- `shape transfer with coefficient renormalization`
- `metric-robust ridge survival`

Framing to avoid:

- `universal transport law`
- `full thermodynamic theory`
- `complete transport closure`
- `geometry-independent law`

## If this were rejected for being too broad, what wording would be narrowed first?

Narrow in this order:

1. Drop `metric-robust` from the headline before dropping `geometry-tested`.
2. Replace `principle` with `tested-family transport organization` if principle language is judged too ambitious.
3. Replace `geometry-tested` with `shape-tested across GF0/GF1/GF2` if the editor wants the tested family named explicitly.
4. Move `falsifiable` into the cover letter or Results framing if the editorial bar for theory language is especially high.

Do not narrow by:

- upgrading the thermodynamic language while weakening the geometry language
- removing the pre-commit qualifier and leaving a broader but less accurate transport claim
- swapping `tested family` for `general` or `universal`

## One-Line Cover-Letter Version

If a cover letter needs one sentence, use:

> The paper identifies a Pareto-like productive ridge, explains it through a falsifiable pre-commit mechanism, shows that the mechanism transfers across a tested geometry family at the level of shape, and shows that the ridge survives a conservative thermodynamic metric upgrade.

## Bottom Line

The novelty is not that the project found a better optimum. The novelty is that it converted a numerical ridge into a scoped transport principle that survives both a first geometry test and a first metric-robustness test.
