## Introduction Arc

The Introduction should answer five questions in order.

1. Why is delayed-memory active transport in gated environments a nontrivial control problem?
2. Why is one-best-parameter optimization too narrow a framing?
3. Why is pre-commit organization the right mechanism level to target first?
4. Why must any strong claim be tested beyond one geometry and beyond one metric?
5. What exact claim level does the present paper support?

## Suggested Paragraph Logic

### Paragraph 1: broad problem and significance

- introduce active transport in complex gated environments as a control problem with delay, memory, and environmental structure
- motivate why gating and geometric complexity create competing objectives: reliability, speed, and cost
- set up the idea that memory can help, but not generically or monotonically

### Paragraph 2: gap in the usual framing

- explain that a simple optimization framing risks over-compressing the problem into one best point
- motivate the need to distinguish structural operating families from single optima
- introduce the idea that productive transport may instead form a competitive manifold or ridge with objective-dependent branches

### Paragraph 3: mechanism gap

- explain that even if a ridge exists, its meaning is unclear without a mechanism-level reduction
- motivate the pre-commit level: before final crossing, trajectories must search, align, dwell, and commit
- argue that this is the natural first level for a reduced transport principle because crossing-completion structure is both later and more geometry-specific

### Paragraph 4: generality gap

- explain that a one-geometry result is insufficient for a principle claim
- explain that a one-metric result is also insufficient, especially when efficiency depends on incomplete bookkeeping
- motivate the need for a first geometry-transfer test and a conservative thermodynamic upgrade

### Paragraph 5: paper contribution and claim hierarchy

- state the strongest current claim: a geometry-tested, thermodynamically upgraded pre-commit transport principle organized around a Pareto-like ridge
- state the strongest safe claim: shape-level universality with coefficient-level renormalization, not full crossing-law or full thermodynamic closure
- state the most important open extension: completion-layer and fuller thermodynamic closure

### Paragraph 6: roadmap paragraph

- map Figures 1-4 to the argument sequence
- Figure 1 defines the problem and transport object
- Figure 2 establishes the Pareto-like ridge quantitatively
- Figure 3 explains the ridge through the pre-commit principle and thermodynamic branch reordering
- Figure 4 shows geometry-tested universality with coefficient-level renormalization

## Why this is more than a gated-maze optimization study

This answer should appear explicitly near the end of the Introduction.

- the paper does not end with one best operating point
- it identifies a Pareto-like ridge with distinct branches
- it explains that ridge through a pre-commit transport principle
- it tests that principle beyond the reference maze
- it shows that the principle survives a metric upgrade

That sequence is what justifies the principle-paper framing.

## What is genuinely general here, and what remains outside scope?

The Introduction should answer this at a high level without overloading the reader.

Genuinely general now:

- the pre-commit backbone as the first decisive transport object
- the roles of delay, memory, and flow in organizing the competitive ridge
- shape-level transfer with coefficient renormalization across the tested family
- persistence of the ridge under a conservative thermodynamic metric upgrade

Outside scope now:

- full crossing-completion law
- coefficient-exact universality
- total entropy production and full thermodynamic closure
- final claims for untested stress-test geometries

## Final Introduction Sentence Target

A good final sentence is:

> We show that productive delayed-memory transport is best understood not as a one-maze optimum but as a geometry-tested, thermodynamically upgraded pre-commit transport principle with explicit scope limits.
