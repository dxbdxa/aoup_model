# Introduction Hook Reset

In constrained non-equilibrium transport, the most visible event is often the final passage through a bottleneck, pore, or gate. That visibility has encouraged a persistent intuition: that transport performance is decided at the barrier itself. Yet performance is judged by competing quantities such as reliability, speed, and cost, and those tradeoffs may be fixed much earlier, during the search, recycling, and organization that precede commitment. A central unresolved question is therefore when a driven transport system actually decides whether it will be reliable, fast, or efficient.

Here we address that question in delayed-memory transport through gated barriers and find that the system does not collapse onto a single best operating point. Instead, across the tested geometry family and within current bookkeeping, productive transport occupies a Pareto-like ridge that is selected before final crossing by a delay-admissible, memory-smoothed, flow-ordered pre-commit backbone. Geometry transfer shows that this backbone survives at the level of shape while coefficients renormalize, and a thermodynamic metric upgrade shows that the ridge persists even when the preferred efficient branch changes. The problem is therefore recast from one of barrier-crossing optimization to one of pre-commit transport selection.
