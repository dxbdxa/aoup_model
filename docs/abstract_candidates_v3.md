## Lead Abstract Candidate

Delayed-memory guidance in complex gated environments is often framed as an optimization problem, but the central question is more structural: what part of productive transport is genuinely general, and what part is specific to one geometry or one efficiency metric? Here we show that productive operation is organized not by a single best point but by a narrow Pareto-like ridge in control space. Confirmatory scans separate success-, efficiency-, and speed-favored operating points while keeping the competitive family confined to a thin delay strip. Refined mechanism analysis and a first pre-commit gate theory show that this ridge is created before final crossing, through a delay-admissible, memory-smoothed, flow-ordered backbone linking search, wall-guided approach, gate-local residence, and commitment. A first geometry-transfer validation then shows that this backbone survives across a tested geometry family at the level of shape, while geometric scales and local coefficients renormalize. Finally, a conservative thermodynamic upgrade shows that the ridge survives more careful cost accounting, even though the preferred efficient branch shifts from a moderate-flow screening optimum toward a fast-completion branch. The current evidence therefore supports a geometry-tested, thermodynamically upgraded pre-commit transport principle with explicit limits on full crossing-law and full thermodynamic closure.

## Alternative Abstract Candidate A

Memory can improve active transport in complex gated environments, but not as a monotone benefit and not as a geometry-specific optimum. Using staged scan localization, confirmatory front analysis, refined mechanism extraction, a first reduced pre-commit theory, geometry transfer, and a conservative thermodynamic upgrade, we show that productive delayed-memory transport is organized by a narrow Pareto-like ridge rather than a single best operating point. The ridge is sharply constrained by delay, occupies a low but non-minimal memory band, and is ordered mainly by flow, which separates success-, efficiency-, and speed-favored branches. Mechanistically, the decisive structure appears before final crossing: productive operation is selected by a pre-commit backbone that converts search and wall-guided approach into gate-local residence and commitment faster than recycling or stale loss can dominate. The first geometry-transfer validation shows that this backbone survives across the tested family at the level of shape, while coefficients renormalize. A thermodynamic metric upgrade preserves the ridge but changes which branch appears most efficient, shifting preference from a moderate-flow screening branch toward a fast-completion branch. These results support a geometry-tested, thermodynamically upgraded pre-commit transport principle, while leaving full crossing-law closure and total entropy production as open extensions.

## Alternative Abstract Candidate B

The productive-memory problem in gated active transport is usually posed as a search for an optimal parameter set, yet such a framing obscures both mechanism and generality. We instead show that the competitive region is an extended Pareto-like ridge with distinct success, efficiency, and speed front tips. Its mechanism is already visible before final crossing: a delay-admissible, memory-smoothed, flow-ordered pre-commit backbone determines whether trajectories reach productive commitment or fall into stale recycling. This principle survives a first geometry-transfer test across the reference maze, single-bottleneck channel, and pore-array strip, but only at the level of shape; geometric scales and local coefficients still renormalize. A conservative thermodynamic upgrade further shows that the ridge persists under stronger efficiency accounting, even though the preferred efficient branch moves from the moderate-flow screening family to a high-flow fast-completion branch. The present work therefore moves beyond one maze and beyond one metric, while remaining scientifically conservative: it supports a geometry-tested, thermodynamically upgraded pre-commit transport principle, but not yet a universal crossing-completion law or a full thermodynamic closure.
