# Abstract Candidates Reset

## Candidate 1: Slightly More Nature-like

In constrained transport, the most visible event is often the final barrier crossing, but it is unclear whether performance is actually selected there or earlier in the dynamics that lead up to commitment. Here we show that delayed-memory transport through gated barriers does not collapse onto a single best operating point. Instead, across the tested geometry family, productive states occupy a Pareto-like ridge that separates reliability, speed, and cost. A coarse-grained pre-commit description shows that this structure is selected before final crossing by a dynamical backbone linking search, wall-guided approach, gate-local residence, and commitment. Within this backbone, delay acts as an admissibility condition, memory is productive only within a smoothing band before it becomes stale, and flow orders which branch favors success, efficiency, or speed. The same backbone survives geometry transfer at the level of shape while coefficients renormalize. The ridge also survives a completion-aware thermodynamic metric upgrade within current bookkeeping, even though the preferred efficient branch changes. These results support a scoped principle: in the tested family, transport performance can be selected before crossing by pre-commit organization rather than at the crossing event itself.

## Candidate 2: Slightly More Nature Physics-like

When a driven system crosses a bottleneck, it is natural to attribute its transport performance to the final passage event. The harder question is whether reliability, speed, and cost are instead fixed earlier by the non-equilibrium dynamics that prepare commitment. Using delayed-memory transport in a tested family of gated geometries, we find that the productive regime is an extended Pareto-like ridge rather than a single optimum. The ridge is confined to a narrow delay strip, ordered mainly by flow, and split into distinct reliability-, efficiency-, and speed-favored branches. A fitted pre-commit reduction shows that this structure is organized by a delay-admissible, memory-smoothed, flow-ordered backbone connecting bulk search, wall-guided search, gate approach, and pre-commit residence before strong commitment. Across the tested geometry family, the backbone survives at the level of shape while coefficients renormalize. Within current bookkeeping, the ridge also persists under a completion-aware drag-normalized metric upgrade, although the preferred efficient branch shifts. The results identify pre-commit organization, rather than final crossing, as the first locus of transport selection in the tested family.

## Candidate 3: Balanced Broad-interest Version

Constrained non-equilibrium transport must balance reliability, speed, and cost, but it is often unclear when that balance is decided. Here we show that, in delayed-memory transport through gated barriers, performance is not first selected at the last crossing step. Instead, across the tested geometry family, the competitive states lie on a Pareto-like ridge that is established before final crossing. Distinct success, efficiency, and speed tips occupy the same narrow delay-admissible band, while flow orders the tradeoff along the ridge. A pre-commit backbone of search, residence, recycling, and commitment dynamics explains this early selection and identifies memory as beneficial only when it smooths the pre-commit cycle without becoming stale. The backbone shape survives geometry transfer, although coefficients renormalize, and the ridge survives a thermodynamic metric upgrade within current bookkeeping even though branch preference changes. The resulting picture is a scoped transport principle: in the tested family, performance can be selected by pre-commit dynamical organization before completion becomes visible in final-crossing statistics.
