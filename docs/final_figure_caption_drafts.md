## Figure 1

Figure 1 | Problem setup, reference scales, and the pre-commit transport object. A gated transport geometry defines the reference scales `l_g` and `tau_g` and anchors the nondimensional controls `Pi_m = tau_mem / tau_g`, `Pi_f = tau_f / tau_g`, and `Pi_U = U / v0`. The key reduced object is the pre-commit backbone linking bulk search, wall-guided search, gate approach, gate-local residence, and commitment. Post-commit crossing is shown only as a deferred downstream layer, so the paper enters the Results through the transport object that is already supported by the current evidence.

## Figure 2

Figure 2 | Quantitative discovery of the Pareto-like productive ridge. The confirmatory scan resolves the productive region into an extended non-dominated ridge rather than a single optimum. The competitive family remains confined to a thin `Pi_f` strip, while success, efficiency, and speed occupy distinct front tips with negligible top-10 overlap. The figure therefore establishes the structural discovery that the rest of the paper explains: productive operation is a branched ridge in control space, not a one-point optimum.

## Figure 3

Figure 3 | Central evidence for the Pareto-like ridge and the pre-commit principle. Canonical success, efficiency, speed, balanced, and stale operating points define the named branches used for mechanism tests. The intervention-validated reduced law then identifies which control parameter acts on which part of the backbone first: delay and flow act first on the pre-commit timing budget, memory acts more cleanly on approach-to-residence organization, `commit_given_residence` remains comparatively flat as a late correlate, and trap burden appears mainly as a punctate stale-edge flag. The matched balanced-versus-stale comparison shows that stale degradation first appears in pre-commit timing and trap burden rather than in a special post-residence commitment conversion. Figure 3 is therefore the main Results figure linking ridge structure, front-tip separation, and pre-commit mechanism.

## Figure 4

Figure 4 | Scope figure: geometry-tested transfer, metric-robust ridge survival, and explicit limits. Across the tested family and within current bookkeeping, the pre-commit backbone transfers at the level of shape across `GF0`, `GF1`, and `GF2`, while geometric scales and local coefficients renormalize rather than collapsing exactly. The productive ridge also survives the current metric family, even though the preferred efficient branch shifts under completion-aware metrics. The scope boundary panel makes explicit what is supported, what is supported only with qualifier, what is ruled out, and what remains out of scope. This figure therefore converts the paper from a one-maze, one-metric story into a tested-family, scope-explicit principle paper without promoting any unsupported completion-law or full-closure claim.

## Caption Policy

Use the following caption rules consistently:

- Figures 1 and 2 may state the numerical and object-level claims directly.
- Figure 3 may state the mechanism claim directly because it is now a completed Results package.
- Figure 4 should carry the standing qualifier `across the tested family and within current bookkeeping` explicitly.
- No caption should imply full crossing-law transfer, coefficient-exact universality, or full thermodynamic closure.

## What belongs in the caption versus the main text

Safe in captions:

- Pareto-like ridge rather than single optimum
- pre-commit backbone
- shape transfer with coefficient renormalization
- ridge survival under metric refinement

Keep in main text or Discussion rather than in captions:

- broader universality speculation
- completion-layer extrapolation
- full thermodynamic-closure language
- stronger generality claims for untested geometries

## Bottom Line

The captions should reinforce the final hierarchy:

- Figure 2: discovery
- Figure 3: central mechanism evidence
- Figure 4: scope and qualifier

That is the shortest route from image to claim without overshooting the completed evidence.
